Section 2: Property Documents & Agreements

Required Documents

Do you have these documents ready to provide to the buyer?

Why these are needed:

Buyers have a legal right to see these documents before signing a contract. They show what's officially registered about your property and any restrictions or rights that come with it.

Where to get them:

  • Title search: Order from Queensland Titles Office online or through your solicitor
  • Survey plan: Usually comes with title search, or from Titles Office

Cost: Around $30-50 total for both documents

Property Agreements & Restrictions

Are there any ongoing agreements or arrangements that will affect the new owner?

What we're looking for:

Any deals, agreements, or arrangements you've made that the new owner will need to know about or continue.

Examples:

  • Tenant living in the property
  • Neighbor using part of your driveway
  • Agreement to maintain shared fence
  • Someone else's pipes/cables crossing your land
  • Informal arrangements about property use

What type of agreements exist?

Government/Utility Rights

Common examples:

  • Power lines crossing your property
  • Water/sewer pipes under your land
  • Council drainage easements
  • NBN/telecommunications equipment

Check for: Easements listed on your title search or council notices you've received

Rental History

Has this property been rented out in the last 12 months?

Why this matters:

Queensland law says rent can only be increased once every 12 months. If you recently increased rent, the new owner can't increase it again until the 12 months is up.

Section 3: Planning, Environment & Land Use

Property Zoning

Do you know what your property is zoned for?

What is zoning?

Zoning determines what you can legally do with your property - like run a business, build extensions, or use it for short-term rentals (Airbnb).

Common zoning types:

  • Residential (houses, units)
  • Commercial (shops, offices)
  • Industrial (factories, warehouses)
  • Mixed Use (combination)
  • Rural (farming, large blocks)

Where to find it:

  • Council rates notice
  • Council website property search
  • Planning certificate from council
  • Ask your solicitor

Examples: Low Density Residential, Neighbourhood Centre, General Industrial

What You Are NOT Required to Investigate

You do NOT need to research or provide information about these topics:

Flooding & Natural Hazards

You are not required to research flood history, natural disaster risks, or climate hazards. Only declare if you have personal experience of flooding/damage on your property.

Planning Restrictions

You are not required to research council building restrictions, development potential, business use rules, or short-term rental regulations. Only declare what you know from personal experience.

Vegetation & Wildlife

You are not required to research vegetation clearing rules, koala habitat restrictions, or protected species requirements. Only declare known restrictions or notices you have received.

Have you done any building work yourself in the last 6 years?

You must declare if you did:

  • Building work yourself as the main contractor
  • Major renovations or extensions

What you must do:

Section 6: Body Corporate Information

🏠 Not Applicable

Scheme Type

What type of body corporate scheme is your property in?

Community Titles Scheme (newer properties):

  • Most apartments, units, and townhouses built after 1997
  • Gated communities and residential complexes
  • Properties with shared facilities

BUGTA Scheme (older properties):

  • Unit complexes built before 1997
  • Older strata-title properties
  • Building Units and Group Titles Act properties

How to tell:

  • Check your title documents
  • Look at body corporate meeting notices
  • Ask your body corporate manager
  • Contact your solicitor if unsure

Community Titles Scheme Documents

Community Management Statement

Do you have the current community management statement?

What is this document:

The community management statement is the key legal document for your scheme. It contains:

  • Lot entitlements (your share of costs and voting rights)
  • By-laws that everyone must follow
  • Exclusive use areas (like car parks assigned to you)
  • Body corporate rules and procedures

Where to get it:

  • Body corporate manager
  • Queensland Titles Office
  • Your solicitor
  • Sometimes available from real estate agent

Body Corporate Certificate

Do you have a current body corporate certificate for your lot?

What this certificate contains:

  • Outstanding body corporate fees
  • Special levies or upcoming major expenses
  • Insurance details
  • By-law breaches or disputes
  • Financial health of the body corporate

How recent: Certificate should be no more than 3 months old for property sales

Cost: Usually $200-400 from your body corporate manager
